Track 23: Investor-Grade Mobility Infrastructure Memo (v2)

Positioning: An inflation-resilient, asset-backed, cash-flow-generating urban mobility infrastructure play in Lagos, Nigeria.

1. Executive Summary

Track 23 is a structured, data-driven commercial transport platform designed to formalize high-demand Lagos corridors currently served by fragmented informal operators. The model integrates fleet control, cashless fare systems, route intelligence, and investor-grade financial reporting.

  • Year 1 focus: operational proof on priority corridors with strict dispatch reliability and leakage suppression.
  • Year 3 focus: multi-hub Lagos scale with route-level profitability and enterprise contracts.
  • Year 5 focus: city-wide platform scale and interstate replication readiness.

2. Problem Statement

  • Urban mobility demand exceeds organized bus supply, creating chronic delays and commuter friction.
  • Manual cash collections produce revenue leakages and weak treasury controls.
  • Informal dispatch creates inconsistent headways, lower utilization, and poor customer trust.
  • Safety, compliance, and maintenance practices are uneven and difficult to audit.
  • Fuel and FX volatility destabilize operator margins without dynamic pricing systems.

3. Market Opportunity (TAM, SAM, SOM)

Sizing Framework

  • TAM: total annual passenger spend on Lagos bus/minibus commuting.
  • SAM: high-density corridors Track 23 can operate under formal permits and hub coverage.
  • SOM: bottom-up capture from active buses x trips x occupancy x fare yield.

Underwriting Principle

Investment decisions should rely on bottom-up SOM with corridor-level unit economics, while TAM/SAM supports strategic upside and market depth.

4. Business Model

Revenue Streams

  • Passenger fare revenue (core)
  • Corporate commuter contracts and seat blocks
  • In-bus and hub advertising inventory
  • Digital payments and wallet/USSD processing economics
  • Data and reporting services for enterprise/regulatory stakeholders

Commercial Logic

  • Route-specific fare bands with peak/off-peak controls
  • Fuel-indexed fare review framework to preserve real margins
  • Fleet rotation to balance mileage and extend asset life

5. Technology Infrastructure

  • Admin dashboard: route P&L, load factor, punctuality, incidents, compliance metrics
  • Driver management: KYC, shift allocation, behavior scoring, sanctions and incentives
  • Route intelligence: headway optimization, congestion-aware dispatching, deadhead reduction
  • Revenue tracking: cashless ticketing, reconciliations, anomaly detection, audit trails
  • Passenger access: USSD-first ticketing plus QR/smartcard and optional mobile app

6. Operational Structure

  • Fleet acquisition: blended purchase, lease-to-own, and asset-SPV financing
  • Hub model: central workshop plus distributed depots by corridor cluster
  • Driver incentives: fixed base + punctuality bonus + safety bonus - violation deductions
  • Fuel control: closed-loop fuel cards, telematics variance checks, route-adjusted norms
  • Maintenance control: preventive intervals, approved vendors, parts traceability

8. Expansion Plan

  • Year 1-2: Lagos corridor consolidation and KPI discipline
  • Year 3: multi-hub Lagos scale and enterprise contracts
  • Year 4: multi-investor fleet/depot SPV partnerships
  • Year 5: interstate replication of the Lagos operating system

9. Risk Mitigation

  • Inflation/FX: indexed pricing, local sourcing, critical parts inventory buffers
  • Regulatory: permit-first operations and compliance-grade reporting interfaces
  • Fuel volatility: surcharge bands, efficiency controls, phased CNG/e-mobility pilots
  • Execution: phased deployment with hurdle-based capex release gates

10. Exit Strategy

  • Strategic sale to mobility or logistics platform
  • Private equity or infrastructure fund recapitalization
  • Secondary liquidity at HoldCo/SPV level after cash-flow stabilization
  • Potential public listing path post multi-city operational maturity
